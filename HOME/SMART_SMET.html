SMART vs. SMET: A Deeper Look

SMART goals are the cornerstone of many coaching approaches. They're designed to be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound. However, in today’s dynamic world, a slightly more nuanced approach is gaining traction: SMET.

SMET expands upon SMART by adding an Evaluate component. This isn't just about checking off a box; it's about actively reflecting on the process, identifying roadblocks, and adjusting strategies as needed. This iterative approach is vital for sustained progress, particularly when dealing with complex goals that encompass the interplay of body, mind, and spirit.

Think of it this way:

  • SMART: Plan, Execute, Achieve. (Linear)
  • SMET: Plan, Execute, Evaluate, Transform (Iterative)

Summary

SMART Goals

  • Specific: Well-defined and clear.
  • Measurable: Quantifiable; you can track progress.
  • Achievable: Realistic and attainable.
  • Relevant: Aligned with your broader values and goals.
  • Time-Bound: Has a defined deadline.

SMET Goals

  • Specific: Well-defined and clear.
  • Measurable: Quantifiable; you can track progress.
  • Achievable: Realistic and attainable.
  • Relevant: Aligned with your broader values and goals.
  • Time-Bound: Has a defined deadline.
  • Evaluate: Regularly assess progress, identify challenges, and adjust your plan.
  • Transform: Adapt your approach based on evaluation to ensure ongoing growth.

Final Explanation: SMART vs. SMET - Which to Choose?

Generally, SMET is preferable, particularly when working with Body, Mind, and Spirit. Here’s why:

  • Holistic Growth: Body, Mind, and Spirit are interconnected. SMET's "Evaluate" and "Transform" steps explicitly account for the dynamic interplay between these aspects. For instance, a fitness goal might need to be adjusted if stress levels are high (mind/spirit) or if physical limitations arise (body).
  • Resilience: Life throws curveballs. The ability to evaluate progress and adapt your plan is crucial for maintaining momentum and overcoming obstacles.
  • Self-Awareness: The "Evaluate" step encourages deep introspection, fostering a greater understanding of your strengths, weaknesses, and patterns of behavior. This is vital for sustainable change.
  • Long-Term Success: SMET promotes a growth mindset, recognizing that progress is often nonlinear and that setbacks are learning opportunities.

When might SMART be sufficient?

  • For very simple, straightforward goals with minimal potential for disruption (e.g., "Drink 8 glasses of water each day").
  • As a starting point before transitioning to a more adaptive framework.
